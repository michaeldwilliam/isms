Each phase produces records that feed the next, and together they form the management system an auditor certifies. Below is that journey worked through one fictional UK fintech. Highlighted documents are the spine of the engagement; every supporting record links through as well.

01
Phase 1 · Cl. 4.1–4.3

Define the boundary

Before anything else, agree exactly what the certificate will cover — which systems, people, locations and data — and who and what it must answer to.

The first document an auditor opens. It draws a precise line around what the certificate covers, and what is deliberately left out and why. Get this wrong and everything after it is measured against the wrong boundary.

The engine of the ISMS. Each threat is scored before and after controls on a 5×5 scale, against a defined acceptance threshold, so leadership can see exactly where residual risk still sits.

Most companies don't chase ISO 27001 for fun. Usually a big client demanded it, a regulator flagged a problem, or something went wrong and the question could no longer be ignored. The work itself is manageable once you know the standard, but there's a lot of it. Monarch Consolidated Ltd is a made-up UK fintech I built to show what a real engagement produces, from the opening health check through to the records that keep the system running.

The documents build on each other. The scope draws the boundary. The health check sets the baseline. The risk list ranks the dangers and the action plan assigns the fixes. The applicability statement records which controls are in use. Then the supporting records — the asset list, the objectives, the internal audit, the corrective actions, the management review — turn a one-off assessment into a management system an auditor can certify. Each one is built to stand on its own and survive a close look from an outside inspector.
